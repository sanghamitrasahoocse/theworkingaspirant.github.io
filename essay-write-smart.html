How this sits next to Essay Writing (ESSELGO)

Essay Writing — the ESSELGO Framework → is a single unified algorithm: one sequence for generating and structuring any essay from a blank page. This page is a different kind of tool — six ready-made page-by-page skeletons to drop a topic into once you already know its shape. In practice they can slot together: use ESSELGO's early steps to plan, then pick one of the six approaches below to actually lay out the body pages. Where both pages touch the same idea — dimensional reading of a philosophical topic, connector phrases, fact-anchoring to the SDGs — treat them as two independent worked versions of it, not a single merged rule.

Read This First

The Universal Blueprint

Every one of the six approaches below slots into the same page-by-page skeleton. Learn this once — it doesn't change no matter which approach you pick on exam day.

A. What UPSC actually rewards

The essay syllabus names exactly three things it pays for. Almost every rule below exists to serve one of these:

  1. Staying closely to the topic — not adjacent to it, not a launchpad for a memorised answer.
  2. Effective and exact expression — grammar, clarity, no ambiguity.
  3. Flow / cohesion — one page reads as the continuation of the last, not a new list item.
Language is not the most important thing. Flow is.

B. The word budget, page by page

  • Total length: 1000–1200 words — never under 1000, never over 1200.
  • Per page: roughly 100–120 words, but this depends entirely on your own handwriting — count your own average before Mains, don't assume.
  • Per paragraph: never exceed 60 words (50–60 is the working range; the conclusion is the one exception — see 1G).

C. The 10-page skeleton

Pg 1Intro + History + Overview
Pg 2–7/8Body — one dimension per page
Pg 8/9World View
Pg 9Anti-view / Limitations
Pg 10Solutions + Conclusion

General pacing rule inside the body: change dimension roughly every 1–1.5 pages so no single idea overstays.

D. Page 1, line by line

  • Introduction (≤60 words). Whichever type you choose, it must contain the topic's own keywords in the first couple of lines — not arrive at them on line five.
  • Historical background (≤60 words). A real historical or current-affairs anchor, explicitly linked to the topic's exact wording — not a stand-alone history lesson.
  • Essay overview (~20 words). "Let us now discuss…" — this is what tells the examiner your structure before they've read three pages guessing at it. Don't reveal every point here; keep some cards for later pages.
Seven intro types worth having ready
Real or hypothetical anecdote Quote-based Thinker / context-based Definition-based Contextual / keyword-meaning Prose or poem opening Historical opening
Treat the examiner like a five-year-old — plate everything for them.

E. The world-view page — non-negotiable

Even for a fully India-centric topic, one page (2–3 paragraphs) must show how the theme plays out beyond India — usually one country/region that "got it right" and one that hasn't (or should, right now).

Why this rule exists

A first attempt scored only 110 in essay — a poor score — for one specific reason: the entire essay was written purely from India's perspective, with no world view at all. That single omission was expensive enough to become a permanent rule in this method.

F. The anti-view page — and when it isn't one

  • Clear-stance topic: dedicate one page (~page 9) to genuine limitations or counter-considerations. Rough proportion: eight pages in favour, one page of limits — never flip this into an essay that argues mostly against its own topic.
  • Genuinely debatable topic: don't squeeze both sides onto one page. Split the whole main body across both sides instead (e.g. four pages each) — a single "anti-view" page can't do justice to a topic that's contested by design.
  • Self-contained topics: some topics already argue both extremes inside the approach itself (see the Sectoral worked example below) — in that case, don't force a redundant separate anti-view page. Decide this in the 20-minute planning phase, not mid-essay.

G. Solutions, conclusion, and the circle of life

  • Never end on the anti-view's negative note — pivot to solutions before you conclude.
  • Two valid configurations: (1) if solutions were saved for the end, open the last page with them, then conclude; (2) if solutions were already threaded through every body page, skip straight to the conclusion.
  • Circle of life: whatever opened your introduction — anecdote, quote, thinker, prose — must reappear in the conclusion. No loose ends.
  • Conclusion length is the one exception to the 60-word ceiling — stretch to 75–100 words if that's what it takes to land the punch line.
  • Signal phrases: "To conclude," "In a nutshell," "Finally," "Having discussed various aspects, we can conclude that…"
Named conclusion device: The Ostrich

An ostrich buries its head in the sand rather than face trouble. Close with something like: "We can either be like an ostrich and bury our heads in the sand, or tackle the challenges head-on with the solutions discussed above." Works for any essay that followed limitations-on-one-page → solutions-on-the-next. A separate library of roughly fifteen conclusion themes (Ostrich is one) is referenced as living on the module's own blog under "essay strategy" — worth tracking down separately for the full set.

H. Flow beats facts beats flowery language

The explicit hierarchy taught: cohesion > factual precision > ornate vocabulary. Two illustrative examples from the instructor's own attempts:

Flow can survive a factual error

An essay on "competition as boon or bane for youth" was structured entirely around age-brackets from the National Youth Policy — brackets that turned out, on checking afterward, to be factually wrong. The essay still scored 150, the highest of that attempt's two essays — because the structural flow held from the first line to the last.

But flow can't rescue a missing dimension

Contrast this with 1E above: strong flow saved an essay with a wrong fact, but no amount of flow saved the essay that skipped the world-view page entirely. Structure protects against small slips; it doesn't excuse skipped requirements.

Practical fix for weak English: read newspaper editorial pages daily (the left-hand column, specifically) rather than chase vocabulary lists.

I. View + example, not just view

Pure opinion has too many lossy translation steps: you think it, mentally translate it, commit it to paper — and then the examiner interprets it their own way. By the end, several rounds of drift can leave the examiner reading something you never quite meant, and even a well-understood view might just not match theirs.

The fix — split every paragraph roughly in half

~30 words of view + ~30 words of a concrete example, data point, scheme, or report. Across seven body pages at two examples per page, that's 14 examples backing 14 views. Even if the examiner disagrees with four or five views, the rest — and the evidence itself — still earns marks.

  • Good data anchors by theme: surveys / Budget announcements (general), IPCC (environment), UN / UNDP (world view), ARC (polity / administration).
  • For philosophical topics specifically: alternate ~30 words philosophy → GS-grounded example → philosophy → another example. This keeps a philosophical essay from drifting into pure opinion.
  • Avoid political figures as examples, anywhere in Essay or Ethics — the examiner's own leanings are unknown, and both papers reward demonstrated neutrality.
  • Vary where solutions come from page to page — don't let every page's solution read like recycled Ethics language. Pull from the GS paper that actually owns that dimension (GS-1 society vocabulary for a family page, GS-2 governance tools for a polity page, and so on) so eight pages don't all sound like the same answer restated.

J. Presentation & expression — the non-negotiables

  • No cuttings or strike-throughs — signals an unclear thought process to the examiner.
  • No unintroduced short forms — spell the full term out once, then abbreviate. Sustainable Development Goals, then SDGs from there on.
  • Zero grammar and spelling errors — non-negotiable precisely because the word budget is tight; fix through daily editorial reading, not a vocabulary drive.
  • Indent every paragraph; leave visible white space between paragraphs.
  • Underline at most one line per paragraph — whichever line you instinctively feel is strongest (a fact, a scheme name, a phrase). Underlining everything cancels the effect.
  • Blue pen for the essay body; black or gel pen for underlining.
  • No headings, no diagrams inside the essay — a personal-preference rule from this method (nicknamed after Aviral Ganga, the "uninterrupted flow" theme of Namami Gange), followed for unbroken cohesion. Not a universal UPSC requirement — plenty of topper copies do use headings. Your call.
  • Time budget: ~20 minutes to plan structure, ~70 minutes to write ~10 pages ≈ 7 minutes/page — roughly double the per-page time available on a GS answer. None of the above is optional given that much time.

K. Connectors — implicit vs explicit

Every page break needs a bridge or the "cohesion" criterion suffers.

Implicit (harder, more elegant)

End a paragraph on a word or idea that naturally becomes the next paragraph's subject — e.g. end on "women", open the next on the gender pay gap; end on "policy", open the next page as Polity/Administration.

Explicit (easier, always safe)

A short linking sentence: "Having discussed the sociological dimension, let us now move to the economic…" or "Expanding our view to the technological dimension, we observe that…"

Rule of thumb: reach for the implicit link first. The moment it feels forced, just write the explicit sentence — don't burn brainstorm time hunting for a clever bridge.

Writing the Essay

The Six Approaches

Pick the topic's own logic, not the other way around. You don't need all six in your working toolkit — most aspirants land on 3–4 they're genuinely comfortable with (see Part 3).

I

PESTEL (a.k.a. STABLE)

One dimension per page — the default everyone already knows

Sociological → Economic → Political → Technological → Environmental → Legal, one per page. The first three are fixed in order; the last three get reordered to fit the topic.

Pg 1Intro/Hist/Overview
Pg 2Sociological
Pg 3Economic
Pg 4Political
Pg 5Technological
Pg 6Environmental
Pg 7Legal
Pg 8World View
Pg 9Anti-view
Pg 10Solutions+Concl.
Worked example
Topic

"Dreams which should not let India sleep"

Sub-dimension chaining inside the Sociological page: Child → Girl Child → Women → Youth — each paragraph seeds the next one's subject, which is what makes a page feel connected rather than listy.

Full connector chain demonstrated across the body: Women (gender pay gap) → Economic (job creation, gig economy) → ends on "policy" → Political/Administration (policy formulation, bureaucratic execution) → ends on "e-governance" → Technological → tech's environmental fallout → Environmental → ends on "climate justice" → Legal.

World view: Africa — resource-rich, potential-rich, currently "sleeping." Anti-view: genuine limitations/challenges (no true opposing case for this topic). Conclusion: the Ostrich theme fits directly, since limitations sit on page 9 and solutions on page 10.

Use with caution

Flagged as the single most over-used, "exhausted" approach among the six. Treat it as the fallback once the other five genuinely don't fit a topic — not the default you reach for first.

II

Chronological (Level Approach)

Widen the topic across levels of scale

Individual → Family → Private-sector Organisation → Public-sector/Government → Society → Nation → World. A level can run to 1.5 pages if it needs to — nothing is fixed to exactly one page.

Pg 1Intro/Hist/Overview
Pg 2Individual
Pg 3Family
Pg 4Private Org.
Pg 5Public/Govt.
Pg 6Society
Pg 7Nation
Pg 8World View
Pg 9Anti-view
Pg 10Conclusion*
Worked example
Topic — UPSC 2018

"A people that values its privileges above its principles loses both."

Per-page theme (this approach needs an explicit theme layered on top of the level, unlike PESTEL where the dimension itself is the theme): Para 1 = the issue this level faces when privilege eclipses principle, backed by a real example, not just a view; Para 2 = the resulting loss + level-specific solutions.

Family-level nuance worth remembering: the same thing — emotional or financial support, say — is a principle when it's mutual, and a misused privilege when it curdles into one-sided over-dependence.

Solutions varied by level (never recycled Ethics language): Individual → values / madhyam marg; Family → GS-1 society vocabulary (interdependence, kinship, no gendered roles); Private sector → shared organisational purpose, e.g. a Tata-style common-goal culture (deliberately avoiding Ethics jargon like "ends" here); Government → GS-2 governance tools (Citizen's Charter, social audit, MGNREGA as example).

Anti-view: legitimate cases where upholding a privilege actually serves a principle — sovereignty, parliamentary privilege, a bureaucrat's administrative discretion.

*Since solutions are already distributed across every level's page, page 10 opens straight into the conclusion — no separate solutions section needed.

Explicitly not taught here

The thesis–antithesis structure is flagged as a trap for this kind of topic — candidates get tangled, repeat themselves, and can't sustain the justification. Not part of this toolkit.

III

SDG Approach

One Sustainable Development Goal per page

Built on a simple observation: a typical multi-dimensional essay already uses ~14–16 "dimensions," and there are exactly 17 SDGs. Each page follows a fixed two-part theme — Issue → Solution — explicitly tied back to the topic's own core phrase every single time.

Pg 3SDG 5

Worked example
Topic — UPSC 2017

"The destiny of a nation is shaped in its classrooms."

Reading tip baked into the topic itself: "classroom" is deliberately broad — school, college, university, vocational/skill centres, even civil-service training academies all count.

SDG 1 (No Poverty) page — Issue: poverty as a persistent, structural drag on national potential (a strong response linked it to Preamble ideals and the Multidimensional Poverty Index). Solution: classrooms (widely defined) breaking the inter-generational poverty cycle — cite Samagra Shiksha, PM SHRI, Mid-Day Meal/PM POSHAN, Mudra loans, MSME schemes, NSQF, NEP 2020, and Amartya Sen's Capability Approach as a concept-anchor.

SDG 5 (Gender Equality) page — connected with an explicit connector ("Let us now discuss the topic with respect to the issue of gender equality"). Strong data anchors: Iceland/Finland's HDI–gender-equality correlation, India's Child Sex Ratio (919 per 1000 boys, Census 2011) even in well-educated states, WEF Gender Gap Index (India 64.1% vs. global average 68.8%) — plus a note to include the third-gender dimension, not just the binary.

World view: Japan (education visibly shaping national discipline/character) vs. under-resourced systems generally, e.g. Pakistan/Afghanistan (education "treated like a beggar," no functioning system). Anti-view options: real constraints teachers/schools face (from a Value Education–style session, or the GS-2 education chapter) — or the broader point that it's never only the classroom: peers, social media, family values, and cultural/linguistic gaps all compete with what a classroom teaches.

Best use-case

The "rescue" approach. If you're 3–4 pages into an essay built on another approach (say Socio → Economic → Polity) and run out of natural dimensions, switching into SDG-per-page for the remaining pages is a legitimate, even recommended, mid-essay pivot.

IV

Sectoral Approach

Build each page on a GS-syllabus sector

Each page is built directly on a sector you've already studied in GS, rather than an abstract dimension — which forces the essay to stay example-driven and GS-grounded. This is the direct fix for philosophical topics where a pure "view-based" essay risks not matching the examiner's own view.

Sector bank, by GS paper
GS-IArt & culture, religion, architecture/design, health, education, geo-politics/geography, sports, media & journalism, population/social sector
GS-IILegal, administrative, political, policy, media, tourism
GS-IIIEconomy, agriculture, science & tech, security, space, defence
Pg 1Intro/Hist/Overview
Pg 2Sector 1
Pg 3–53–4 more sectors
Pg 6World View
Pg 7Solutions+Concl.

Trade-off to know going in: sectoral essays cover fewer dimensions than SDG essays in the same page count — roughly 4–5 sectors (each needs three paragraphs, ~1.5 pages) vs. 6–7 SDGs (two paragraphs each).

Worked example
Topic

"Philosophy of wantlessness is utopian, while materialism is a chimera"

Unpacking the topic (a reusable model for opening any philosophical topic): wantlessness = total absence of desire = an impractical ideal; materialism = unchecked consumerism = an illusion that never actually satisfies; the real answer sits in the middle — Aristotle's Golden Mean, or the Buddhist Madhyam Marg.

Per-sector theme (three paragraphs, 40–50 words each): Para 1 wantlessness-as-issue, Para 2 materialism-as-issue, Para 3 the middle path as solution.

Tourism sector, fully worked: wantlessness taken literally = no tourism at all, to preserve pristine spaces (Lakshadweep vs. a heavily commercialised Andaman & Nicobar) — impractical. Materialism unchecked = over-commercialisation (overcrowding, waste mismanagement, fishery decline) — an illusion, unsustainable. Middle path = eco-tourism, tied back to the Rigvedic idea of Rita (cosmic order/harmony between nature and humanity).

Connector technique specific to this approach: end a paragraph on a word that pivots the sector — ending on "policy" naturally opens an administrative/political sector next; ending on "scientific solutions" opens a technological sector next. Described as a practiced skill, not a knowledge one.

World view: Bhutan (wantlessness-driven) vs. USA (materialism-driven), then a middle-path read for countries generally.

On the anti-view page here

Topics like this one already argue both extremes inside the main body by design — so there may be no separate anti-view page needed at all. Recognise this during the planning phase, and let the body run through to wherever it naturally ends (here, page 7–8).

V

Metaphor Approach

Read one word in the topic as a symbol, then "type" it

Some topics contain a word that's meant to be read as a metaphor, not literally — e.g. "Ships do not sink because of the water around them; ships sink because of the water that enters them" (ship, water), or "The time to repair the roof is when the sun is shining" (roof). Once you've spotted the metaphor, invent categories/"types" of it to structure the essay.

Two tiers of categories
Easy tier

Sectoral-flavoured types — economic roof, education roof, defence roof, health roof, unemployment roof, corruption roof. Usable, but unremarkable.

Fresh tier (what this module pushes for)

Emotional/mental-health, familial-ties, mutual-interdependence, cultural, character, diplomatic, internal-security, urbanisation, demographic-dividend. Pick 5–7 that genuinely fit; you don't need all nine.

Why "emotional/mental health" is a strong current pick: it keeps surfacing in the Budget, the Economic Survey, and Parliament, and in high-profile public cases — and UPSC's last direct mental-health ask was a 2020 workplace case study, so it's considered due for a return.

Pg 1Intro/Hist/Overview
Pg 2–8One "type" per page (5–7)
Pg 9World View
Pg 10Anti-view
Pg 11Conclusion*
Page 1, live-drafted model (the "roof" topic)
Overview line modelled in class

"Let us now discuss the topic and see how various 'roofs' need to be repaired while the sun is shining." (~20 words)

A strong anecdote structure: a short hypothetical story — a place facing a slow-building problem, someone acting early, and the exact metaphor line landing in their mouth or the narration. Story + example + explicit link to the metaphor phrase, inside ~65–70 words (treated as an acceptable stretch beyond the usual 60-word ceiling, for an anecdote intro specifically).

One page, fully worked — Emotional Roof

Para 1: the issue, pitched as "not yet grave" — a strain building quietly.
Para 2: consequences if left unaddressed, in a deliberately clock-is-ticking register (stress, family breakdown, productivity loss, suicide risk) — immediately followed by the pivot to government solutions (ManoDarpan, Tele-MANAS). Skipping the "what happens if you don't fix this" half and jumping straight to solutions was flagged as a common, avoidable miss.

Connector chain across a full body

Emotional (ends on "contentment at home") → Family (ends on "value education for children") → Cultural (Westernisation eroding tradition) → Character → [switch to an explicit connector here — the implicit link stops flowing naturally] → Diplomacy → Security → Demographic Dividend → Urbanisation.

World view (three paragraphs this time): a country that repaired its roof in time (Japan/Scandinavia/Singapore), one that never did (Pakistan), one that should start now (Africa). Anti-view: "reform is continuous," not a single moment you either catch or miss — illustrated with COVID-19 (India's health system was unprepared, yet strengthened reactively mid-crisis) and 1991 (crisis-as-opportunity, Aapda ko Avsar).

*Solutions are already threaded through every page, so skip straight to the conclusion — loop back to the Page 1 anecdote.

The one hard rule specific to this approach

Exactly one "type" per page. This topic-shape tempts you to over-write a single dimension (mental health especially) — resist it, or the other 5–6 roofs won't fit inside the 1000–1200 word budget.

VI

Contemporary-Relevance Approach

Zoom to named, current Indian issues

Zoom from macro dimensions down to specific, named, current issues — which deliberately forces an example into every paragraph. Built for anyone who tends to drift into pure opinion. The narrowest-use approach of the six: a last-resort tool for when you're stuck 3–4 pages in, not a default.

Worked example
Topic

"Crises faced in India: Moral and Economic"

The topic's own wording supplies the structure: Moral / Economic / Both / Other — and the "Other" bucket doubles as the anti-view, since it proves the topic can't be reduced to just the two named categories.

Pg 2–3Moral
Pg 4–5Economic

Classification: Moral → communalism, marriage/values debates, women's safety. Economic → urbanisation, unemployment. Moral + Economic → cyber-fraud, pollution (both a values angle and a monetary cost). Other → terrorism, tariffs/taxation, diplomatic or linguistic conflict.

Before the Exam

Choosing an Approach in the Exam Hall

The core reframe

You don't choose an approach in the exam — you pre-choose, through practice, which 3–4 of these six actually suit your own writing style, and drill only those beforehand. In the exam hall the only live decision left is which topic fits one of your pre-chosen approaches. A well-practiced candidate should be able to fit at least one of their chosen approaches onto virtually any topic on the paper — being stuck on structure shouldn't happen if this prep is done properly.

Topic selection is the harder skill

A cautionary example: one candidate committed to a topic, wrote two to three pages, realised mid-essay he couldn't sustain it, crossed it all out, and restarted on a new topic — and scored very low. The crossed-out attempt itself told the examiner the candidate lacked clarity, before a single new word was even read.

Two essays, two different approaches
  • Never reuse the same approach for both essays in one sitting — it reads as a single memorised template rather than genuine range.
  • Vary the intro style too — don't run an anecdotal intro in both essays; pair it with a quote-based, keyword-based, or contextual intro in the second.
  • Approaches can be mixed within a single essay: open with Socio → Economic → Polity, then pivot into SDG-per-page once the natural PESTEL dimensions run dry. Several body pages can quietly change methodology as long as the flow holds.
Why this paper is worth the effort

Cited as evidence of how much this paper can move a rank: Harshita Goyal (AIR 2 that year) scored the all-India-highest 144 in Essay as this faculty's student, and the same year's Rank 1 scored 141 in Ethics. Essay and Ethics are framed as two of the highest-leverage papers for a candidate willing to invest in structure.

Closing the Loop

Closing Craft Notes

  • Paragraph count per page isn't fixed by any UPSC rule. Two paragraphs on one page, three on the next is fine as long as the flow holds. Personal preference in this method is to not break a chosen theme mid-essay — but that's a preference, not a rule.
  • Don't walk in with pre-written, topic-agnostic notes on favourite subjects (education, women's issues, etc.) and force every topic to fit them. Let the topic's actual demand pull from memory — not the reverse.
  • Don't second-guess the topic by imagining what everyone else in the hall might write. Pick the one you're personally most comfortable executing.
  • Avoid clichéd, overused examples in both Essay and Ethics — the module's own example is reaching for Gandhi or Ashok Khemka on every third page. Fresher examples read as more genuine.
  • Practice volume is confidence-dependent, not fixed. The instructor's own account: just one full essay written across two entire prep cycles, on the strength of confidence in the method. Standing advice for someone newer to this: 5–6 full essays, roughly one a week, in the run-up to Mains.
  • Topper copies are worth reading for structure, not content. Harshita Goyal's copies were the one named reference point — read them for how she built pages, not what she wrote on them.
  • Mining a historical-background paragraph on demand — a reusable model for almost any "repair it before it's too late" topic: the interwar years between WWI and WWII, where unaddressed crises (rising dictatorships among them) fed directly into the next war. A reminder that GS/History content already on hand is almost always enough — the remaining task is just pointing it at the topic's exact wording.
