Polity Decoded · Chapter 1 · Memory Aids

Historical background — stories, mnemonics and memory hooks

Sanghamitra S.  ·  June 2026  ·  Use before revision · 15 min read

Facts read are facts forgotten. Facts encoded through story, image, or sound are facts retrieved under pressure. This page encodes everything in Chapter 1 — through narrative chains, acronyms, visual flow, number anchors, and association pegs — so the Acts, features, firsts, and numbers stay with you when it matters most.

Use this page after you have read the Complete Overview at least once. Memory techniques work best when there is already something to anchor them to. Come back here before each revision cycle — even five minutes with this page is worth more than re-reading the chapter passively.

Part 1 — The Story: how to remember all 13 Acts in order

Narrative chain

The single biggest problem with Chapter 1 is sequence — aspirants mix up which Act came when, and which feature belongs to which year. The story below gives every Act a character and a cause-and-effect link to the next. Read it once slowly. Then close your eyes and retell it.

A Regulator arrives in 1773 and finds a company running wild — he appoints a Governor-General (Hastings), drags Bombay and Madras into line, and builds a Supreme Court in Calcutta. But the Court immediately picks fights with everyone — so in 1781, an Amending Act steps in and draws a clear boundary: the Court can judge Calcutta's residents, but keep away from the Company's revenue business and official acts.

Three years later, Pitt's India Act (1784) arrives because the Company has become a political monster masquerading as a trading house. Pitt splits the beast in two: the Board of Control handles politics, the Court of Directors handles commerce. Double government — but at least it's honest about it. For the first time, the land is called "British possessions in India."

In 1786, Lord Cornwallis refuses the job of Governor-General unless he gets two things: the power to override his own Council, and the role of Commander-in-Chief. Both are given — the Act of 1786 is essentially a personalised contract.

The Charter Act of 1793 tidies up — it extends that overriding power to all future Governor-Generals, locks in another twenty years of the Company's trading monopoly, and — here's the sting — decides that the British supervisors watching over India will be paid from Indian revenue. India pays for its own supervision.

Twenty years later, the Charter Act of 1813 breaks the trading monopoly wide open — all British merchants can now trade in India. Missionaries are let in. Western education gets a budget. The Company keeps only its tea and China monopoly.

Then comes the big moment: the Charter Act of 1833 completes the centralisation project. The Governor-General of Bengal becomes the Governor-General of India — Bentinck is first. Bombay and Madras lose all legislative powers. The Company stops trading altogether. India is now governed as a unified British territory.

The Charter Act of 1853 quietly does something revolutionary — it separates the legislature from the executive for the first time. A separate legislative council is created, functioning like a mini-Parliament. Open competition for civil services begins. Local provincial governments get seats in the central legislature. And unlike all previous Charters, this one sets no time limit — a signal that the Company's days are numbered.

They end in 1857. After the great Revolt, the British Crown steps in. The Government of India Act 1858 abolishes the Company, abolishes the Board of Control, and creates a Viceroy — Lord Canning, the first. A Secretary of State for India sits in London, accountable to Parliament. A 15-member Council of India assists him. Nothing changes for Indians — only the machinery of British control in England is reformed.

To keep Indians cooperative after the shock of 1857, the Indian Councils Act 1861 offers a gesture — Indians are nominated to the Viceroy's expanded council. Not elected. Nominated. Canning picks three. Bombay and Madras get their legislative powers back. The portfolio system is formalised. The Viceroy can issue ordinances for six months in an emergency.

By 1892, the gesture is slightly more generous — the Indian Councils Act 1892 lets councils discuss the budget and ask questions. Non-official members start arriving through recommendation — election without the word.

Then 1909 — the Morley-Minto Reforms — and the councils dramatically expand (16 to 60 at the centre). An Indian finally sits on the executive council — Satyendra Prasad Sinha, as Law Member. But the price paid is catastrophic: separate electorates for Muslims. Lord Minto becomes the Father of Communal Electorate.

The Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms of 1919 go further — dyarchy splits provinces into transferred subjects (with ministers accountable to elected legislators) and reserved subjects (still with the Governor). Bicameralism and direct elections arrive at the centre. Three Indians must sit on the Viceroy's executive council. The communal electorate expands to Sikhs, Christians, Anglo-Indians, Europeans. The experiment largely fails — ministers have responsibility without power.

The Government of India Act 1935 is the grand finale of colonial legislation — 321 sections, 10 schedules. Dyarchy dies in provinces; provincial autonomy is born. Three lists divide powers between centre and provinces. The All-India Federation is proposed but never born — princely states refuse to join. A Federal Court appears. The Reserve Bank is created. Orissa and Sind become new provinces. Burma leaves. Fourteen per cent of Indians can vote.

Finally — Indian Independence Act 1947. The Mountbatten Plan. Two dominions. British paramountcy lapses. Viceroy's office abolished. The Constituent Assembly can repeal any British Act — including the Independence Act itself. At midnight on August 15, 1947, the story that began with a trading company in 1608 ends with a sovereign people.

Part 3 — Peg system: the Firsts

Visual association pegs

For each "first," a vivid association image. The stranger the image, the better it sticks. Read the image, close your eyes, and see it happen.

First Governor-General of Bengal
Lord Warren Hastings · 1773
Picture Warren Buffett (Waren → Warren) hastily (Hastings) signing papers in Bengal — he is in a rush because it's 1773 and the Regulating Act just arrived.
First Governor-General of India
Lord William Bentinck · 1833
Picture a bent ink pen (Bentinck) writing "Governor-General of INDIA" in 1833 — crossing out "Bengal" because the whole country is now under one pen.
First Viceroy of India
Lord Canning · 1858
Picture someone canning (preserving jars) the last remnants of the Company in 1858 — sealing it shut forever. The canner becomes the Viceroy.
First Indian in Viceroy's Executive Council
Satyendra Prasad Sinha · Law Member · 1909
Picture Satyendra ("Satya" = truth) speaking truth in the council chamber in 1909, sitting in the Law seat. He is the only Indian in the room — and he knows it.
First Governor-General of independent India
Lord Mountbatten · 1947
Picture a mountain (Mount-batten) with a battery (batten) attached — it is August 15, 1947, the mountain is flying the Indian flag, and the battery is powering the new nation.
First Prime Minister of independent India
Jawaharlal Nehru · sworn in by Mountbatten
Picture Nehru's iconic red rose (jawaharlal = jewel) being pinned on by Mountbatten (the mountain) at midnight — the jewel of the nation, sworn in by the mountain.
Father of Communal Electorate
Lord Minto · 1909
Picture a mint (Minto) being split into two halves — one Hindu, one Muslim. The man splitting it in 1909 is the Father of the Split.
First Supreme Court
Calcutta · 1774 · under the Regulating Act
Picture a calculator (Calcutta) with a supreme court gavel on it, dated 1774. The Regulating Act pressed the button that turned it on.

Part 4 — Number anchors: every critical number in the chapter

Number-story anchors
Numbers that UPSC picks — anchored to a story or image
1774
Year Supreme Court established at Calcutta
1773 + 1 year to build = 1774. The Regulating Act came first, the Court followed one year later.
15
Members of the Council of India (1858 Act)
India + 1 = 15. The Secretary of State for India needed 15 advisors — one for each major concern of a nation.
6
Months — life of a Viceroy's ordinance (1861 Act)
Half a year. Emergency powers that last exactly half a year — long enough to govern, short enough to check.
16→60
Central legislative council size before and after 1909 Act
Sweet sixteen grew up to sixty. The Morley-Minto Reforms turned a small room into a hall.
321
Sections in the Government of India Act 1935
3-2-1 like a countdown — because after 321 sections, independence was the next step.
10
Schedules in the Government of India Act 1935
321 sections + 10 schedules. Remember: our Constitution has 12 schedules — 1935 had 10. Two more were added at independence.
59·54·36
Items in Federal, Provincial, Concurrent lists (1935 Act)
"Federal has the most, Provincial is next, Concurrent is smallest." 59 → 54 → 36. Descending order. Centre had the longest list.
14%
Population that got voting rights under 1935 Act
Only 14 in every 100 Indians could vote under the British. Universal adult franchise came only with the Constitution of 1950.
120
Members of Chamber of Princes (Narendra Mandal) — 1921
108 princes + 12 representatives = 120. Think of 108 as the sacred number — all 108 princely states had their own prince; 12 represented the rest.
1926
Year Central Public Service Commission set up
1919 Act provided for it; 1926 is when it actually happened. Seven years between the law and the institution — typical of colonial administration.

Part 5 — Visual flow: the power transfer arc

Visual flowchart

The entire chapter is one story of power moving from Company → Crown → Indians → Sovereign Republic. This flow encodes that movement visually. Trace it with your finger once.

Company Rule (1773–1858) — Power consolidated at the top
1773 First control over Company. GG of Bengal created.
→
1784 Double govt. Board of Control. "British possessions."
→
1833 GG of India. Company stops trading. Centralisation peak.
→
1853 Legislature separated. Open competition. No time limit.
1857 Revolt — Crown takes over
Crown Rule (1858–1947) — Power gradually transferred to Indians
1858 Viceroy. Secretary of State. 15-member Council.
→
1861 Indians nominated. Portfolio system. Ordinance power.
→
1909 Indians in executive. Communal electorate. 16 → 60.
→
1919 Dyarchy. Direct elections. Bicameralism. 3 Indians in council.
Simon Commission 1927 → Round Tables → White Paper → 1935
End game (1935–1950) — Full sovereignty achieved
1935 Provincial autonomy. 3 lists. Federal Court. RBI. 321 sections.
→
1947 Independence. Two dominions. Viceroy gone. Sovereignty complete.
→
1950 Constitution in force. Republic declared. The arc is complete.

How to use this page in your revision cycle

First read: Read the narrative chain in full (Part 1). Take 10 minutes. This builds the skeleton.

Same day: Read the acronyms aloud — one per Act. Say the word, then say what each letter stands for. Do not write yet.

Next day: Cover the acronym meanings and test yourself. Then do the peg images — close your eyes and see each one.

Third day: Run the rapid recall drill (Part 7). Any that you miss, return to that section only.

Every revision cycle: The story chain first, then the drill. The flow chart is for the night before the exam — one glance, five minutes, and the whole chapter's arc is in your head.
