How many polity questions does UPSC actually ask? A 15-year analysis

Sanghamitra S.  ·  June 2026  ·  6 min read

Before you open Laxmikanth, there is one question worth settling: how much does polity actually matter in the exam? Fifteen years of data gives a clear answer — and it is more consistent than most aspirants realise.

One of the most common mistakes I see working professionals make is treating polity as a side subject — something to skim through between Current Affairs and History. The numbers tell a very different story.

The range — 6 to 22 questions — is the critical number here. You cannot predict which end of that range your exam year will fall on. That unpredictability is precisely why you must prepare the full breadth of the subject, rather than selectively covering what seems important in a given year.

At 2 marks per question since 2011, a year with 20 polity questions means 40 marks — nearly a tenth of the entire Prelims paper — resting on this one subject. That is not a side subject. That is an anchor.

Mains: the picture is even clearer

Since 2013, UPSC has dedicated an entire paper — GS Paper II — to Governance, Constitution, Polity, Social Justice and International Relations, carrying 250 marks in total. Of that, polity-specific questions have consistently accounted for 110 to 125 marks in recent years.

This consistency from 2018 onwards is a signal, not a coincidence. UPSC has settled into a stable pattern of testing this subject heavily and repeatedly. For a working professional deciding where to invest limited study hours, that stability makes the decision straightforward.

Three things this data tells the working aspirant

I

Do not skip chapters. The variance in question count — 6 one year, 22 the next — means UPSC draws from the full subject unpredictably. Chapters that seem peripheral in one year become central the next. The only safe preparation is complete preparation.

II

Polity rewards revision more than most subjects. Unlike Current Affairs, the core content is stable year on year. Every hour you spend revising it compounds across both Prelims and Mains. Time invested here has a longer shelf life.

III

Mains demands understanding, not recall. 110 to 125 marks in Mains cannot be answered by memorising provisions. UPSC expects you to analyse, compare, and apply constitutional logic — which changes how you should read the subject from your very first sitting.
