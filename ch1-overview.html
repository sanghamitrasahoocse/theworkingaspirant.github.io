Historical Background of the Indian Constitution

From trading company to constitutional republic — the 350-year journey that shaped India's governance.

Overview Mind Maps Prelims Edition Mains Edition

Why this chapter matters

Every constitutional provision India has today — federal structure, legislative councils, civil services, fundamental rights — has a root in the acts covered in this chapter. Understanding these acts is not history. It is the foundation of everything else in Polity.

Prelims: 2–4 questions per year directly from this chapter. Mains: essential context for GS2 questions on constitutional evolution and federal structure.

The Big Picture

The British came to India in 1608 as traders under the East India Company, which held an exclusive trading charter from Queen Elizabeth I since 1600. For over 150 years they remained primarily a commercial enterprise. The shift began in 1765 when the Company obtained the 'Diwani' — rights over revenue and civil justice — of Bengal, Bihar and Orissa. This was the moment a trading company became a territorial power.

From 1765 to 1947, India's governance evolved through two broad phases — Company Rule and Crown Rule — each marked by a series of Acts of the British Parliament that progressively shaped the structure of administration, legislation, and eventually the Constitution itself.

Two phases at a glance

🏢 Company Rule (1773–1858) — East India Company governed India under increasing British Parliamentary oversight

👑 Crown Rule (1858–1947) — British Crown governed India directly after the Sepoy Mutiny of 1857

Phase 1 — Company Rule (1773–1858)

The Company Rule phase produced seven landmark Acts. Each Act responded to the failures of the previous one — collectively building, piece by piece, the architecture of centralised governance that the Indian Constitution would eventually inherit.

1773

Regulating Act of 1773

First British attempt to control the Company. Created the Governor-General of Bengal (Warren Hastings — first holder), made Bombay and Madras subordinate to Bengal, established the Supreme Court at Calcutta. Foundation of central administration in India.

1781

Amending Act of 1781 (Act of Settlement)

Fixed defects of the Regulating Act. Exempted Governor-General and Company servants from Supreme Court jurisdiction for official acts. Excluded revenue matters from Supreme Court. Appeals from Provincial Courts to go to Governor-General-in-Council, not Supreme Court.

1784

Pitt's India Act of 1784

Distinguished commercial and political functions of the Company. Created Board of Control for political affairs alongside existing Court of Directors (commercial). Established double government. First time Company's territories called 'British possessions in India'.

1786

Act of 1786

Enacted specifically for Lord Cornwallis — gave Governor-General power to override Council decisions in special cases, and allowed him to also serve as Commander-in-Chief simultaneously.

1793

Charter Act of 1793

Extended overriding powers to all future Governor-Generals. Extended Company's trade monopoly for 20 more years. Board of Control members to be paid from Indian revenues.

1813

Charter Act of 1813

Abolished Company's trade monopoly (except tea and China trade). Asserted British Crown's sovereignty over Company territories. Allowed Christian missionaries and western education. Local governments empowered to impose taxes.

1833

Charter Act of 1833

Final step in centralisation. Governor-General of Bengal became Governor-General of India (Lord William Bentick — first). Governors of Bombay and Madras lost legislative powers. Company ended as commercial body — became purely administrative. First attempt at open competition for civil servants (blocked by Court of Directors).

1853

Charter Act of 1853

Last Charter Act. Separated legislative and executive functions for the first time — created Indian (Central) Legislative Council (mini-Parliament). Introduced open competition for civil services (Macaulay Committee appointed 1854). First local representation in Central Legislative Council. Did not fix a period for Company's rule — signal that it could end any time.

The thread running through Company Rule

Each Act progressively centralised power — from three independent presidencies to one Governor-General of India. Each Act also progressively increased British Parliamentary oversight over the Company. By 1853, the Company was already a hollow administrative shell. 1857 simply ended it.

Phase 2 — Crown Rule (1858–1947)

The Sepoy Mutiny of 1857 ended Company Rule entirely. In 1858, the British Crown assumed direct responsibility for governing India — beginning the Crown Rule phase that produced a series of Government of India Acts, each expanding Indian participation in governance, until Independence in 1947.

1858

Government of India Act of 1858

Abolished the East India Company. India now governed in the name of the Crown. Governor-General renamed Viceroy — Lord Canning was first. Ended double government (abolished Board of Control and Court of Directors). Created Secretary of State for India (Cabinet member) with a 15-member advisory Council of India. The Act changed the machinery of control in England but did not substantially alter governance in India.

1861

Indian Councils Act of 1861

First step toward representative institutions — Indians nominated to Viceroy's legislative council (Raja of Benaras, Maharaja of Patiala, Sir Dinkar Rao in 1862). Reversed centralisation — restored legislative powers to Bombay and Madras. Established new legislative councils for Bengal, North-Western Provinces and Punjab. Recognised portfolio system (introduced by Canning in 1859). Viceroy empowered to issue ordinances during emergencies (6-month validity).

1892

Indian Councils Act of 1892

Increased non-official members in Central and provincial legislative councils. Gave councils power to discuss budget and ask questions to executive. Introduced indirect elections (called "nomination on recommendation") — word 'election' not used. Limited but significant step toward representative government.

1909

Indian Councils Act of 1909 (Morley-Minto Reforms)

Increased Central legislative council from 16 to 60 members. First Indian in Viceroy's Executive Council — Satyendra Prasad Sinha as Law Member. Introduced communal representation for Muslims via separate electorate — Lord Minto became 'Father of Communal Electorate'. Extended separate representation to presidency corporations, chambers of commerce, universities and zamindars.

1919

Government of India Act of 1919 (Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms)

First declaration of objective — responsible government in India (August 1917). Introduced dyarchy in provinces — subjects divided into transferred (Ministers responsible to legislature) and reserved (Governor and Executive Council, not responsible to legislature). Introduced bicameralism — Upper House (Council of State) and Lower House (Legislative Assembly). First direct elections. Extended communal electorates to Sikhs, Indian Christians, Anglo-Indians, Europeans. Set up Central Public Service Commission (1926). Simon Commission appointed 1927 — all British members, boycotted by all parties.

1935

Government of India Act of 1935

Longest Act — 321 Sections, 10 Schedules. Proposed All-India Federation (never came into being — princely states refused to join). Three lists: Federal (59 items), Provincial (54 items), Concurrent (36 items) — residuary powers with Viceroy. Abolished dyarchy in provinces, introduced provincial autonomy. Introduced dyarchy at Centre (never implemented). Bicameralism in 6 provinces. Extended franchise to 14% of population. Established Reserve Bank of India (1935), Federal Court (1937), Federal Public Service Commission. Abolished Council of India. Separated Burma from India. Created Orissa and Sind as new provinces.

1947

Indian Independence Act of 1947

Clement Attlee declared end of British rule (February 20, 1947). Mountbatten Plan accepted by Congress and Muslim League. Act ended British rule from August 15, 1947. Partitioned India into two dominions — India and Pakistan. Abolished office of Viceroy — replaced by Governor-General for each dominion. Empowered Constituent Assemblies to frame constitutions and repeal any British Act. Abolished Secretary of State for India. Proclaimed lapse of British paramountcy over princely states. Constituent Assembly of India (1946) became Parliament of Indian Dominion. Lord Mountbatten — first Governor-General of India. Jawaharlal Nehru — first Prime Minister.

The thread running through Crown Rule (complete)

1858 — Changed machinery, not governance. 1861 — First Indians in legislature. 1892 — First indirect elections. 1909 — Communal electorates introduced. 1919 — Dyarchy, direct elections, bicameralism. 1935 — Provincial autonomy, federal structure blueprint. 1947 — Independence.

The 1935 Act is especially significant — its federal structure, three lists, and provincial autonomy directly shaped the Indian Constitution of 1950.

Legacy — what the Constitution inherited

Federal structure with three lists → from GoI Act 1935

Bicameralism at Centre → from GoI Act 1919

Civil services structure → from Charter Act 1853 and 1919

Emergency powers of the President → from GoI Act 1935

Office of CAG → from GoI Act 1935

Writ jurisdiction of courts → from GoI Act 1935

The thread running through Crown Rule (so far)

Each Act progressively expanded Indian participation — from nominated Indians (1861) to indirect elections (1892) to communal electorates (1909) to direct elections and dyarchy (1919). The direction was always toward responsible government, but always with the British retaining ultimate control.
