The Core Philosophy

This page exists because of one explicit passage in the book, not an inference I made on your behalf.

From the book's own FAQ — "How should I practice answer-writing using this book?"

"GS Answer-writing is nothing but miniature essays about very pin-pointed topics and with a narrower focus. The techniques of introduction, narrative building, Should Things, creating conclusions are squarely applicable while writing answers for GS as well as Optional exercises."

Four techniques, named directly — not my summary, his list. Everything in this vault's answer-writing coverage either is one of these four, or is a second, separately-confirmed passage extending a specific essay-tool to GS, Ethics, or Optional papers.

1
Introduction — the 4Q² method, confirmed elsewhere as "widely applicable to all the papers of the Mains exam including Ethics, Essay, and General Studies."
2
Narrative building — the same sequenced, block-by-block flow that carries an essay also carries an answer, just compressed.
3
Should Things — the solutions block, built for suggesting concrete fixes rather than just describing a problem.
4
Conclusions — SCoReF's four-part close, same shape, scaled to a shorter answer.

One more explicit line, worth holding onto

"Remember that all the questions in the Mains exam are of the analytical type even though the question may be asked in different paper — be it GS, optional subject or Ethics." No anecdotes, no emotional appeal, no personal voice — the same impersonal, argument-first register the Essay paper demands applies to every GS, Ethics, and Optional answer too.

Confirmed, Not Assumed

What Transfers Directly

Five tools from the Essay Writing vault, each with its own explicit line in the book extending it to GS, Ethics, or Optional answers. Full explanations live on that page — this is what's specifically confirmed here.

1
Confirmed · Ethics paper
Deconstruction
"Deconstruction works remarkably well on Essay topics and Questions which appear in the UPSC Mains exam, including the Ethics paper." Split the question into Thing 1, Thing 2, and the relationship — before answering it as a whole. See the full method on Essay Writing.
2
Confirmed · Ethics, Essay, GS
4Q² — the Opening Line
"The 4Q2 is widely applicable to all the papers of the Mains exam including Ethics, Essay, and General Studies." What is Thing 1, what is Thing 2, what connects them, why does it matter — compressed into a single opening line instead of a full paragraph. Full method on Essay Writing.
3
Confirmed · GS, Optional, Ethics
The Four Lenses (Indian Angle)
"You can use this cheat code for GS, Optional subjects and Ethics" — the book's own line about its Indian-angle technique. Women, Environment, Weaker Sections, Economic Growth — four dependable ways to ground any answer in India specifically. Full breakdown on Essay Writing.
4
Confirmed · Essay, Ethics, GS
The Five Levers (Solutions)
"Generally applicable to almost all questions in Essay, Ethics, and GS papers" — the solutions-block technique. Law/Regulation, Technology, Psychology, Inclusiveness, Environment: five reliable places to find a concrete "should" when a question asks for one. Full breakdown on Essay Writing.
5
Confirmed · GS, Ethics, Optional
The Fact-Cheat Approach
The book states its fact-sourcing method "will help improve your score in General Studies, Ethics and optional papers." Anchor your fact-bank to the UN SDGs and Indian government reports, never Wikipedia, and always attach a fact to the specific point it's proving. Full version on Essay Writing.

A safer approach than trusting this diagram blindly

Pull five of your own highest-scoring 150-word and 250-word answers and count how many distinct points each actually carries. That gives you a block-count calibrated to your own writing density, not a generic estimate — and it's a five-minute exercise against material you already have.

Reading the Question

The Four Question Types

The book states the Mains exam's questions sort into four types — and that the four body-blocks (Bad · Good · Indian · Should) exist to answer exactly these four types. Practicing the blocks is, by the book's own logic, indirect practice for these questions.

Question typeWhat it's really askingNearest body-block
Knowledge-based"What is X?"Sets up before Bad/Good Things
Impact-based"What is/will be the impact of X on Y?"Bad Things & Good Things
Solution-based"What is being done / can be done about X?"Should Things

Before You Write

Rough Work for Answers

The same principle as Schematization on the Essay page, sized down — a 150-word answer needs a plan, just a much smaller one.

Why it still matters at this scale
It's tempting to skip planning for a "short" 150-word answer — but with that little room, a single misplaced or missing point costs a noticeably larger share of the marks than it would in a 1000-word essay. A 30-second point-list beats writing straight onto the answer sheet.
The compressed version
Deconstruct (Thing 1 / Thing 2 / relationship) in one line each. List your points in the sequence you'll write them — not the order they occur to you. Number them. Write straight from the numbered list, don't re-plan mid-answer.
