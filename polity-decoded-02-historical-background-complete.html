Historical background of the Constitution — the complete picture

Sanghamitra S.  ·  June 2026  ·  Complete reference · 20 min read

Our Constitution did not arrive in 1950 from nowhere. It was the end point of 350 years of gradual, often reluctant, constitutional evolution — from a trading company's charter to the world's longest written constitution. This post covers the full chapter: every Act, every standalone event, every footnote, and both historical cabinet tables.

The British came to India in 1608 as traders, in the form of the East India Company, which held the exclusive right of trading in India under a charter granted by Queen Elizabeth I in 1600. In 1765 the Company obtained the diwani — rights over revenue and civil justice — of Bengal, Bihar and Orissa, and began its career as a territorial power. In 1858, in the wake of the Sepoy Mutiny, the British Crown assumed direct responsibility for the governance of India. This rule continued until India was granted independence on August 15, 1947.

With Independence came the need for a Constitution. A Constituent Assembly was formed in 1946, and on January 26, 1950, the Constitution came into being. But various features of the Indian Constitution and polity have their roots in British rule — in a series of Acts and events that laid down the legal framework for governance and administration in British India. These are covered below in chronological order under two headings: Company Rule (1773–1858) and Crown Rule (1858–1947).

The so what of this chapter: every major feature of our Constitution — federalism, parliamentary government, separation of powers, the civil services, even the emergency provisions — has a direct ancestor in the colonial Acts. You cannot understand what we built without knowing what we inherited.

Company Rule · 1773–1858

1773

Regulating Act

First step toward central administration

Of great constitutional importance as: (a) the first step taken by the British Government to control and regulate the affairs of the East India Company; (b) the first recognition of the Company's political and administrative functions; and (c) it laid the foundations of central administration in India. It designated the Governor of Bengal as Governor-General of Bengal, with an Executive Council of four members — Lord Warren Hastings was the first. It made Governors of Bombay and Madras subordinate to the Governor-General of Bengal, unlike earlier when the three presidencies were independent. It provided for the establishment of a Supreme Court at Calcutta (1774) comprising one Chief Justice and three other judges. It prohibited Company servants from engaging in private trade or accepting presents or bribes from natives. It strengthened British Government control by requiring the Court of Directors to report on revenue, civil, and military affairs in India.

1781

Amending Act — also known as Act of Settlement

Fixing the jurisdictional conflict created by 1773

Passed to rectify defects of the Regulating Act. It exempted the Governor-General, Council members, and Company servants from the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court for official acts. It excluded revenue matters from the Court's jurisdiction entirely. It provided that the Supreme Court had jurisdiction over all inhabitants of Calcutta, and required it to administer personal law of defendants — Hindus tried under Hindu law, Muslims under Mohammedan law. It laid down that appeals from Provincial Courts went to the Governor-General-in-Council, not the Supreme Court. It empowered the Governor-General-in-Council to frame regulations for Provincial Courts and Councils.

1784

Pitt's India Act

Double government — commerce vs. politics separated

Distinguished between the commercial and political functions of the Company. Allowed the Court of Directors to manage commercial affairs, but created a new body — the Board of Control — to manage political affairs. Established a system of double government. Empowered the Board of Control to supervise and direct all civil, military, and revenue operations of British possessions in India. Significant for two reasons: first, Company territories were for the first time called "British possessions in India"; second, the British Government was given supreme control over the Company's affairs and administration in India.

1786

Act of 1786

Enacted to meet Lord Cornwallis's two demands

When Lord Cornwallis was appointed as Governor-General of Bengal in 1786, he placed two demands before accepting the post: that he be given power to override the decision of his Council in special cases, and that he would also serve as Commander-in-Chief. The Act of 1786 was enacted specifically to make both provisions.

1793

Charter Act

Overriding power extended; Indian revenues fund British oversight

Extended the overriding power given to Lord Cornwallis to all future Governor-Generals and Governors of Presidencies. Gave the Governor-General more powers and control over the subordinate Presidencies of Bombay and Madras. Extended the trade monopoly of the Company in India for another twenty years. Provided that the Commander-in-Chief was not to be a member of the Governor-General's Council unless specifically appointed as such. Laid down that members of the Board of Control and their staff were henceforth to be paid out of Indian revenues.

1813

Charter Act

Trade monopoly abolished; education and missionaries enter

Abolished the trade monopoly of the Company in India — Indian trade thrown open to all British merchants. However, the monopoly over trade in tea and trade with China continued. Asserted the sovereignty of the British Crown over Company territories in India. Allowed Christian missionaries to come to India for the purpose of enlightening the people. Provided for the spread of western education among the inhabitants of British territories in India. Authorised Local Governments to impose taxes on persons and to punish those who did not pay.

1833

Charter Act

Final step toward centralisation — Governor-General of India created

Made the Governor-General of Bengal the Governor-General of India, vested with all civil and military powers over the entire British territory in India — Lord William Bentinck was the first. Deprived the Governors of Bombay and Madras of their legislative powers; the Governor-General of India was given exclusive legislative powers for all of British India. Laws made under previous Acts were called Regulations; laws made under this Act were called Acts. Ended the Company's commercial activities — it became a purely administrative body, holding territories "in trust for His Majesty, His heirs and successors." Attempted to introduce open competition for civil servants and stated that Indians should not be debarred from any office — but this provision was negated by the Court of Directors.

1853

Charter Act

Legislature separated from executive — a constitutional landmark

The last of the series of Charter Acts (1793–1853) and a significant constitutional landmark. It separated, for the first time, the legislative and executive functions of the Governor-General's Council. It provided for the addition of six new members called legislative councillors — establishing a separate Governor-General's Legislative Council (also called the Indian Central Legislative Council). This legislative wing functioned as a mini-Parliament, adopting the same procedures as the British Parliament — legislation was for the first time treated as a special function requiring special machinery and special process. It introduced an open competition system for selection and recruitment of civil servants, throwing the covenanted civil service open to Indians; accordingly, the Macaulay Committee was appointed in 1854. It extended the Company's rule but did not specify any particular period — a clear signal that the Company's rule could be terminated at any time. It introduced, for the first time, local representation in the Central Legislative Council — four of the six new legislative members were appointed by the local governments of Madras, Bombay, Bengal and Agra.

Memory hook — Company Rule

1773 — First control, first Governor-General of Bengal (Hastings), first Supreme Court (Calcutta, 1774).

1781 — Jurisdiction clarified; personal law principle established.

1784 — Double government. Board of Control created. "British possessions in India" — first use of the term.

1786 — Cornwallis's two demands met: overriding power + Commander-in-Chief.

1793 — Overriding power generalised; Indian revenue funds British oversight.

1813 — Trade opened to all British merchants (tea + China monopoly retained); missionaries; western education.

1833 — Governor-General of India (Bentinck). Company stops trading. Centralisation complete.

1853 — Legislature separated from executive. Open competition begins. Local representation starts.

Crown Rule · 1858–1947

1858

Government of India Act — Act for the Good Government of India

Company abolished; Crown takes over; Viceroy created

Enacted in the wake of the Revolt of 1857. Abolished the East India Company and transferred powers of government, territories and revenues to the British Crown. India was henceforth to be governed in the name of Her Majesty. Changed the designation of Governor-General of India to Viceroy of India — the direct representative of the British Crown; Lord Canning became the first Viceroy. Ended the system of double government by abolishing the Board of Control and Court of Directors. Created a new office of Secretary of State for India, vested with complete authority over Indian administration — a member of the British Cabinet, ultimately responsible to the British Parliament. Established a 15-member Council of India to assist the Secretary of State — an advisory body with the Secretary of State as Chairman. Constituted the Secretary of State-in-Council as a body corporate, capable of suing and being sued in India and in England. Note: the Act in no substantial way altered the system of government that prevailed in India — it confined itself to improving the machinery by which Indian Government was superintended and controlled in England.

1861

Indian Councils Act

Indians enter the legislature — as nominated members

An important landmark in constitutional and political history. Made a beginning of representative institutions by associating Indians with the law-making process — the Viceroy was to nominate some Indians as non-official members of his expanded council. In 1862, Lord Canning nominated three Indians: the Raja of Benaras, the Maharaja of Patiala, and Sir Dinkar Rao. Initiated the process of decentralisation by restoring legislative powers to Bombay and Madras — reversing the centralising tendency that had peaked under the Charter Act of 1833. Provided for the establishment of new legislative councils for Bengal (1862), North-Western Provinces (1886), and Punjab (1897). Gave recognition to the portfolio system introduced by Lord Canning in 1859 — under this, a council member was made in-charge of one or more departments and authorised to issue final orders on their behalf. Empowered the Viceroy to issue ordinances, without concurrence of the legislative council, during an emergency — the life of such an ordinance was six months.

1892

Indian Councils Act

Indirect election introduced — without using the word

Increased the number of additional (non-official) members in central and provincial legislative councils, but maintained the official majority. Increased the functions of legislative councils — gave them the power of discussing the budget and addressing questions to the executive. Provided for nomination of non-official members on the recommendation of provincial legislative councils, the Bengal Chamber of Commerce, district boards, municipalities, universities, trade associations, zamindars, and chambers. The Act made a limited and indirect provision for the use of election in filling non-official seats — but the word "election" was not used; the process was described as nomination made on the recommendation of certain bodies.

1909

Indian Councils Act — Morley-Minto Reforms

Communal electorate introduced; Indians enter the executive

Lord Morley was the Secretary of State for India; Lord Minto was the Viceroy. Considerably increased the size of legislative councils — the Central legislative council was raised from 16 to 60 members. Retained official majority in the Central legislative council but allowed non-official majority in provincial legislative councils. Enlarged deliberative functions — members could ask supplementary questions and move resolutions on the budget. Provided, for the first time, for the association of Indians with the executive councils of the Viceroy and Governors — Satyendra Prasad Sinha became the first Indian to join the Viceroy's Executive Council, appointed as Law Member. Introduced a system of communal representation for Muslims by accepting the concept of separate electorate — Muslim members were to be elected only by Muslim voters; Lord Minto came to be known as the Father of Communal Electorate. Also provided for separate representation of presidency corporations, chambers of commerce, universities and zamindars.

1919

Government of India Act — Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms

Dyarchy, bicameralism, and direct elections — all for the first time

Montagu was the Secretary of State; Lord Chelmsford was the Viceroy. The Act came into force in 1921. On August 20, 1917, the British Government had declared, for the first time, that its objective was the gradual introduction of responsible government in India. The Act classified all subjects of administration into central and provincial subjects via Devolution Rules — relaxing central control. It further divided provincial subjects into transferred subjects (public health, education, local self-government, agriculture) — administered by the Governor with ministers responsible to the legislature — and reserved subjects (police, justice, prisons, land revenue, finance) — administered by the Governor and executive council without responsibility to the legislature. This dual scheme was called dyarchy, from the Greek di-arche meaning double rule; the experiment was largely unsuccessful. Introduced, for the first time, bicameralism and direct elections — the Indian Legislative Council was replaced by a bicameral legislature: Upper House (Council of State) and Lower House (Legislative Assembly), with the majority of members chosen by direct election. Required three of the six members of the Viceroy's Executive Council (other than the Commander-in-Chief) to be Indian. Extended communal representation by providing separate electorates for Sikhs, Indian Christians, Anglo-Indians and Europeans. Granted franchise to a limited number of people on the basis of property, tax or other qualifications. Created a new office of High Commissioner for India in London, transferring to him some functions previously performed by the Secretary of State. Provided for the establishment of a public service commission — a Central Public Service Commission was set up in 1926. Separated, for the first time, provincial budgets from the Central budget, authorising provincial legislatures to enact their own budgets. Provided for the appointment of a statutory commission to inquire into and report on its working after ten years — this became the Simon Commission. Proposed the establishment of a Chamber of Princes (Narendra Mandal) — inaugurated in 1921, consisting of 120 members (108 princes + 12 representatives of other states), headed by the Viceroy.

Interlude — Simon Commission (1927) and Communal Award (1932)

1927 Simon Commission

In November 1927 — two years before schedule — the British Government announced the appointment of a seven-member statutory commission under the chairmanship of Sir John Simon to report on the condition of India under its new Constitution. All the members of the commission were British, and hence all parties boycotted the commission.

The commission submitted its report in 1930 and recommended: abolition of dyarchy, extension of responsible government in the provinces, establishment of a federation of British India and princely states, and continuation of communal electorate.

To consider the proposals, the British Government convened three Round Table Conferences of the representatives of the British Government, British India and Indian princely states. On the basis of these discussions, a White Paper on Constitutional Reforms was prepared and submitted for the consideration of the Joint Select Committee of the British Parliament. The recommendations of this committee were incorporated — with certain changes — in the Government of India Act of 1935.

1932 Communal Award and the Poona Pact

In August 1932, Ramsay MacDonald, the British Prime Minister, announced a scheme of representation of the minorities known as the Communal Award. The award not only continued separate electorates for Muslims, Sikhs, Indian Christians, Anglo-Indians and Europeans — but also extended it to the Depressed Classes (Scheduled Castes).

Gandhi was distressed over this extension of the communal representation principle to the Depressed Classes and undertook a fast unto death in Yerawada Jail, Poona, to get the award modified. At last, there was an agreement between the Congress and Dr. B.R. Ambedkar, the leader of the Depressed Classes. The agreement — known as the Poona Pact — retained the Hindu joint electorate and gave reserved seats to the Depressed Classes.

The British Government accepted the Poona Pact and modified the Communal Award in respect of the Depressed Classes — the provisions of separate electorates for Depressed Classes were dropped. The provisions of the Poona Pact were subsequently incorporated in the Government of India Act of 1935. In other words, the composition of the central and provincial legislatures under the 1935 Act was based on this modified Communal Award.

1935

Government of India Act

The blueprint our Constitution most borrowed from

A lengthy and detailed document — 321 sections and 10 schedules. Marked a second milestone towards completely responsible government. Provided for the establishment of an All-India Federation of provinces and princely states — but the federation never came into being as princely states did not join. Divided powers between Centre and units into three lists: Federal List (59 items), Provincial List (54 items), and Concurrent List (36 items); residuary powers vested in the Viceroy. Abolished dyarchy in provinces and introduced provincial autonomy — provinces to act as autonomous units; Governors required to act with the advice of ministers responsible to provincial legislature (came into effect 1937, discontinued 1939). Provided for dyarchy at the Centre — but this provision never came into operation. Introduced bicameralism in six out of eleven provinces: Bengal, Bombay, Madras, Bihar, Assam and United Provinces. Reserved seats for Depressed Classes (Scheduled Castes) and extended special representation to women and labour. Abolished the Council of India established by the 1858 Act; Secretary of State for India provided with a team of advisors instead. Extended franchise — about 14 per cent of the total population got the voting right. Provided for the establishment of a Reserve Bank of India. Provided for a Federal Public Service Commission, Provincial Public Service Commissions, and Joint Public Service Commission for two or more provinces. Provided for the establishment of a Federal Court — set up in 1937. Separated Burma from India. Created two new provinces: Orissa and Sind. Provided for safeguards to protect the interests of minorities. Provided for the establishment of a Federal Railway Authority. Provided for the appointment of an Auditor-General of India to audit the accounts of the federation and the provinces.

1947

Indian Independence Act

The end of British rule

On February 20, 1947, the British Prime Minister Clement Attlee declared that British rule in India would end by June 30, 1948. This was followed by agitation by the Muslim League demanding partition. On June 3, 1947, the British Government made clear that the Constitution framed by the Constituent Assembly could not apply to parts unwilling to accept it. On the same day, Lord Mountbatten, the Viceroy, put forth the partition plan — known as the Mountbatten Plan — which was accepted by the Congress and the Muslim League. The Indian Independence Act (1947) gave immediate effect to this plan. The Act: ended British rule and declared India an independent state from August 15, 1947; provided for partition and the creation of two independent dominions of India and Pakistan with the right to secede from the British Commonwealth; abolished the office of Viceroy and provided, for each dominion, a Governor-General appointed by the British King on the advice of the dominion cabinet — Mountbatten became the first Governor-General of India and swore in Nehru as the first Prime Minister; empowered the Constituent Assemblies of both dominions to frame and adopt any constitution and to repeal any Act of the British Parliament including the Independence Act itself; empowered Constituent Assemblies to legislate for their territories until new constitutions were drafted; abolished the office of Secretary of State for India, transferring functions to the Secretary of State for Commonwealth Affairs; proclaimed the lapse of British paramountcy over Indian princely states and treaty relations with tribal areas from August 15, 1947; granted freedom to princely states to join India, join Pakistan, or remain independent; provided for governance of each dominion by the Government of India Act 1935 until new constitutions were framed; deprived the British Monarch of his right to veto bills — this right was reserved for the Governor-General; designated the Governor-General and provincial Governors as constitutional (nominal) heads, to act on the advice of the respective council of ministers; dropped the title of Emperor of India from the royal titles of the King of England; discontinued the appointment to civil services and reservation of posts by the Secretary of State for India.

The 1935 Act is our Constitution's most direct ancestor — our three legislative lists, the Federal Court that became the Supreme Court, the Reserve Bank, the Public Service Commissions, even the emergency provisions all trace back to it. When you study the Constitution, you are often studying 1935 with modifications.
