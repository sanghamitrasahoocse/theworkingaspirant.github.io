Dimensions of Ethics — Applied/Emerging Issues

  • Clinical trials — Testing new drugs on human subjects before public release — raises consent, exploitation, and informed-consent ethics issues, especially with poor/illiterate participants in India.
  • Designer babies — Genetically selecting traits in embryos (looks, intelligence, disease-resistance) — raises questions of eugenics, equality, and 'playing god'.
  • Surrogacy — A woman carries a pregnancy for another person/couple — ethical concerns around commercial exploitation of poor women's bodies (India restricted commercial surrogacy via the Surrogacy (Regulation) Act, 2021).
  • Cloning — Creating a genetically identical organism — raises ethical concerns on identity, consent, and misuse (reproductive vs therapeutic cloning).
  • GMO / LMO — Genetically Modified / Living Modified Organisms — altered at the genetic level; debate over food safety, biodiversity risk, and corporate control of seeds (e.g. Bt cotton in India).
  • GM crops — Genetically modified crops — same debate as above, often discussed with farmer livelihoods and informed consent.
  • Racism — Discrimination based on race/ethnicity — a recurring example for prejudice, dignity, and equality in ethics answers.
  • Body-shaming — Mocking or criticising someone's physical appearance — used as an example of how social attitudes and media can violate dignity and self-worth.

Ethics in Private vs Public Life

  • Daily habits as integrity markers — Examples like punctuality, cleanliness, simple living (shower, modest house, leather products, tax-paying honestly) are often cited to show how small private habits reflect larger integrity.
  • Superstition — Belief in luck/rituals over reason — used to discuss rational thinking vs blind faith, especially for public officials.
  • Punctuality & loyalty culture (Japan) — Japan is a recurring example of a society where punctuality and institutional loyalty are deeply ingrained values.
  • Extra-marital affairs of public figures — Used to discuss where the line lies between a leader's private morality and their public duty/accountability — a genuinely debated ethics topic, not a settled one.

Professional Ethics — People to Remember

  • Hippocratic Oath — The ethical oath doctors take — classic reference point for medical/professional ethics in any answer.
  • E. Sreedharan — Known as the 'Metro Man' of India — cited for incorruptibility, discipline, and execution integrity (Delhi Metro).
  • L. B. Shastri (Lal Bahadur Shastri) — Former PM cited as an icon of moral integrity — known for personal simplicity and famously taking responsibility (resigning as Railway Minister after a train accident, despite no personal fault).
  • JRD Tata — Industrialist known for ethical business practice and corporate welfare-mindedness, often cited against 'profit-at-any-cost' examples.
  • Narayan Murthy — Infosys co-founder, cited for corporate governance and ethical leadership in Indian business.
  • M. S. Swaminathan — Father of India's Green Revolution — cited for science serving public good with integrity.
  • Verghese Kurien — Father of the White Revolution (Amul/cooperative dairy model) — cited for institution-building with integrity.
  • Hospital professional ethics incident — A recurring UPSC case-study archetype worth keeping in mind: a doctor or hospital administrator facing competing duties (e.g. two critical patients needing simultaneous urgent care, or balancing charitable treatment against the hospital's financial viability) — useful as a generic template for medical professional ethics dilemmas.

Environmental Ethics

  • Amrita Devi — 18th-century Bishnoi woman who sacrificed her life hugging trees to stop them being felled — root example for the later Chipko Movement.
  • Chipko Movement — 1970s movement where villagers (notably women) hugged trees to prevent logging — classic eco-ethics example.
  • Sunderlal Bahuguna — Environmentalist and a key Chipko Movement leader.
  • Jadav Payeng — The 'Forest Man of India' who single-handedly planted a forest on a river island in Assam.
  • Rajendra Singh — 'Waterman of India' — known for water conservation and reviving rivers in Rajasthan.
  • Afroz Shah — Led the world's largest beach clean-up movement at Versova Beach, Mumbai.
  • Tulsi Gowda — 'Encyclopedia of forest' — tribal environmentalist from Karnataka honoured with Padma Shri for tree conservation.
  • Animal trafficking / Sparrow decline / animal testing — Used to discuss biodiversity loss and ethics of animal use in research.
  • PETA — Animal rights organisation — cited in debates on animal testing and welfare.
  • Need vs Greed (Jainism reference) — Jain philosophy of consumption only as per need, not greed — used as a sustainability/ethics frame.
  • EIA / SIA — Environmental Impact Assessment / Social Impact Assessment — mandatory clearances before large projects; cited in development vs environment debates.
  • Niyamgiri — Odisha hills case — tribal (Dongria Kondh) rights vs bauxite mining; landmark case of community consent (Gram Sabha) overriding corporate mining interest.

Role of Family in Inculcating Values

  • Mahavir Singh Phogat — Father of wrestlers Geeta and Babita Phogat (subject of the film Dangal) — example of family shaping discipline and values against social norms.
  • Japan / Israel / Scandinavian family models — Cited as cultural models of strong family-based value transmission.
  • Old-age, gender, respect within family — Used to discuss how family structures teach respect for elders and gender sensitivity (or fail to).
  • Upbringing shaping ideology (contrasting examples) — A useful theme to keep a mental folder for: how the same broad environment (family, nation, faith) can produce very different ideological outcomes depending on the specific values instilled — e.g. contrasting a reform-minded upbringing against a radicalising one.
  • APJ Abdul Kalam on roles that shape a child — Kalam frequently spoke of how a handful of figures around a child — parents, teachers, and community/society — jointly shape values; a generally useful reference point when an answer needs to credit multiple social actors, not just the family, for value formation.
  • Broken families → juvenile delinquency — Used to show the cost of family breakdown on children's value formation.
  • Tagore family / Bose family — Cited as examples of multi-generational families known for intellectual and cultural contribution.
  • Joint family vs nuclear family — Used to discuss changing family structures and their effect on value transmission in modern India.
  • Kapoor family (Bollywood) — Cited as an example of multi-generational legacy and family-transmitted values/culture.

Role of Society in Inculcating Values

8.1 Negative/Regressive Social Influences

  • Consumerism — Excess materialism — cited as eroding simplicity and contentment as values.
  • Individualism / bystander effect — Society becoming indifferent to others' suffering — linked to declining collective responsibility.
  • 'Son of the soil' — Exclusionary regional identity politics — used to discuss parochialism vs constitutional values.
  • Khap Panchayat — Informal caste/community councils, sometimes responsible for honour killings — example of regressive social authority overriding law and individual rights.
  • Honour killing — Murder of family members (often young couples) for 'dishonouring' family/caste norms — extreme example of regressive social values.
  • Untouchability / caste — Persisting caste-based discrimination despite constitutional abolition — core example of social vs constitutional values gap.
  • Manual scavenging — Caste-linked degrading occupation of cleaning human waste by hand, despite being legally banned — example of social attitude lagging behind law.
  • Section 377 — Former law criminalising homosexuality, read down by the Supreme Court in 2018 (Navtej Singh Johar case) — example of evolving social/legal ethics around dignity and identity.
  • Mob lynching — Vigilante violence, often rumour-driven — example of social media misinformation translating into real-world harm.
  • Acceptance of corruption — Normalised tolerance of bribery/corruption in daily life — a social attitude problem, not just an institutional one.
  • NIMBY syndrome — 'Not In My Backyard' — people support a cause in principle but resist it affecting them personally (e.g. supporting waste plants but not nearby).
  • 'Chalta hai' attitude — Casual Indian colloquial attitude of tolerating mediocrity or wrongdoing — cited as a barrier to accountability culture.

8.2 Campaigns, Movements & Social Media as Value-Shapers

  • 'Black is Beautiful' — Movement countering racial beauty stereotypes, asserting dignity for Black identity.
  • 'Happy to Bleed' — Indian social media campaign challenging menstruation taboos.
  • Nirbhaya case / India Against Corruption (IAC) — Two major mass movements: the 2012 Delhi gang-rape case sparking nationwide protest on women's safety, and the IAC anti-corruption movement led by Anna Hazare.
  • Blue Whale, Kiki, Momo challenges — Dangerous online 'challenge' trends targeting youth — example of social media's harmful influence absent regulation/awareness.
  • ISIS — Example of radicalisation via online propaganda and distorted ideology.
  • Brexit / US presidential elections — Cited as examples of how media/social media shapes political attitudes and polarisation at a societal scale.
  • Cigarette, alcohol normalisation — Used to discuss how culture/media normalises harmful habits over time.

8.3 Moral & Political Attitudes — Figures

  • Positive: Angela Merkel, Nelson Mandela, Justin Trudeau — Cited as leaders associated with principled, inclusive, or reconciliatory political conduct.
  • Negative: Trump, Stalin, Hitler, Aurangzeb — Cited as leaders associated with authoritarianism, intolerance, or abuse of power (use carefully and neutrally in answers — UPSC rewards balanced, non-inflammatory framing).
  • Caste-based voting — Example of identity politics overriding policy-based political choice.
  • Internal party democracy (lack of) — Cited as an issue undermining democratic political attitudes within parties themselves.
  • Political funding — Opacity in political donations (e.g. electoral bonds debate) — cited as an integrity issue in the political system.

8.4 Social Influence & Persuasion via Media

  • Social media: Arab Spring — Mass political uprisings across the Middle East/North Africa, organised largely via social media — landmark example of digital mobilisation.
  • Social media: #MeToo, 'selfie with daughter' — #MeToo as global accountability movement; 'Selfie with Daughter' as PM Modi's campaign promoting the girl child.
  • Fit India Movement, #TimesUp — Government and global campaigns using social media for health and accountability messaging respectively.
  • Red Cross/Red Dot campaign — Public health/awareness campaigns leveraging media reach.
  • Movies: Padman, Dangal, Toilet — Ek Prem Katha, Pink — Bollywood films cited for driving social conversation on menstrual hygiene, gender in sport, sanitation, and consent respectively.
  • Documentary: Inside Job — Documentary on the 2008 financial crisis — cited for exposing systemic corporate/financial ethics failure.
  • Books: Silent Spring — Rachel Carson's book that launched the modern environmental movement by exposing pesticide harm.
  • Books: Small Is Beautiful — E. F. Schumacher's book advocating human-scale, sustainable economics over unchecked growth.
  • Books: Poverty and Un-British Rule in India — Dadabhai Naoroji's work introducing the 'Drain of Wealth' theory — economic critique of colonial rule.
  • Books: Communist Manifesto / Hind Swaraj — Marx & Engels' foundational socialist text, and Gandhi's foundational text on self-rule and critique of modern civilisation — both cited as ideology-shaping works.
  • Radio/TV: Satyamev Jayate, 'Darwaza Band' campaign — Aamir Khan's TV show that drove national conversation on social issues; sanitation-focused awareness campaign.
  • Usha Mehta — Freedom fighter who ran a secret underground radio station during the Quit India Movement (1942) — example of media as a tool of resistance and integrity under risk.

Impartiality and Non-Partisanship

  • Akbar — Din-i-Ilahi, Sulh-i-Kul — Akbar's syncretic religious policy and doctrine of 'peace with all' — cited as a historical example of impartial, inclusive governance.
  • Ashoka — Dhamma — Ashoka's post-Kalinga policy of moral governance applied evenly across religious and social lines.
  • Shivaji — Hindu-Muslim — Cited for maintaining an inclusive administration that employed people across religious lines despite the religious framing of his era's politics.
  • Officer favouring political interests for post-retirement gain — A recurring case-study archetype worth keeping a mental tab on: a serving officer subtly aligning decisions with a ruling party's interest in hope of a post-retirement appointment (governorship, tribunal seat, Rajya Sabha nomination) — a real and recurring impartiality risk in Indian administration.
  • Touching feet of a politician — Used as an example of sycophantic behaviour by officials that compromises impartiality.
  • Caste discrimination disrupting a welfare scheme (e.g. Mid-Day Meal) — A recurring real-world pattern worth keeping a mental tab on: food cooked or served by a Dalit worker being refused or boycotted by upper-caste families/students in government school feeding programmes — used to illustrate how caste prejudice undermines impartial public service delivery even when policy itself is caste-blind.
  • Partisanship — British 'divide and rule' — Colonial strategy of exploiting religious/caste divisions to maintain control — historical root example of partisanship's damage.
  • Partisanship — caste-based politics, nepotism — Modern examples of partisanship undermining impartial governance.
  • Partisanship — Governor, Speaker — Constitutional offices expected to act impartially; their politicisation is a recurring GS2/GS4 ethics issue.

Classic Ethical Dilemmas Checklist

  • Law vs Conscience — When following the letter of the law conflicts with what feels morally right.
  • Duty vs Humanity — When official duty conflicts with basic human compassion in a specific situation.
  • Economic vs Environment — Development/growth priorities conflicting with ecological protection.
  • Security vs Privacy — State security needs (surveillance, data collection) conflicting with individual privacy rights.
  • Freedom of Speech vs Law and Order — Right to expression conflicting with public order/safety concerns.
  • Means vs End — Whether a good outcome justifies ethically questionable methods to get there.
  • Profession vs Personal Interest — Professional/official duty conflicting with one's own personal gain or interest.
  • Idealism vs Realism — Holding to principle vs adapting to practical/political constraints.
  • Efficiency vs Quality — Speed and output conflicting with thoroughness and standards.
  • Equity vs Equality — Treating people according to their differing needs (equity) vs treating everyone identically (equality).
  • National vs International Interest — A country's own interest conflicting with global/multilateral obligations.
  • Justice vs Peace — Pursuing accountability/punishment vs prioritising reconciliation and stability.
  • Individual vs Community Rights — An individual's rights conflicting with the interests of the wider community.
  • Doing right things vs Doing things right — Pursuing the correct goal vs following correct process — these can pull in different directions.
  • Confidentiality vs RTI — Duty to keep information confidential conflicting with the public's right to information.
  • Cultural values vs Individual Rights — Community/cultural traditions conflicting with an individual's personal rights and choices.

Laws, Rules, Regulations and Conscience as Sources of Ethical Guidance

  • Gandhi — Chauri Chaura — Gandhi called off the entire Non-Cooperation Movement after the 1922 Chauri Chaura violence, prioritising his conscience-based commitment to non-violence over political momentum — classic example of conscience overriding strategic gain.
  • Cuban Missile Crisis — Vasili Arkhipov — A Soviet naval officer refused to authorise a nuclear torpedo launch during the 1962 crisis despite pressure, arguably preventing nuclear war — textbook example of individual conscience as the last line of ethical defence.
  • Sacrifice as a path to conscience-driven action — A theme worth keeping a mental tab on: cases of individuals or rulers giving up position, comfort, or wealth to act on conscience rather than convenience — a recurring motif across history (rulers abdicating, officials resigning on principle, soldiers risking their lives for civilians).
  • Edward Snowden — PRISM — Exposed the NSA's PRISM mass surveillance programme — cited again here specifically as a conscience-over-law example (broke the law to follow his conscience).
  • Whistleblower (general concept) — Cited as a category — someone who reports wrongdoing from within an organisation, often at personal/professional risk.
  • Pepsi vs Coca-Cola (pesticide controversy) — Refers to the early-2000s controversy in India over pesticide residue found in cola products — an ethics-in-business/regulation example.
  • Vietnam War — Muhammad Ali — Refused the military draft on conscientious-objector grounds, losing his boxing title and facing prison — example of personal conscience defying state law at major personal cost.
  • Big Tobacco Scandal — Refers to a tobacco industry insider (widely dramatised as the Jeffrey Wigand case in the film The Insider) who exposed the industry's knowledge of cigarette harm despite public denial — conscience exposing corporate wrongdoing.
  • K. R. Narayanan — Former President of India, cited here as an example of conscience-driven constitutional conduct.
  • Collective conscience — 2012 Delhi gang rape case — The phrase 'collective conscience of society' is a legal concept Indian courts have invoked in sentencing reasoning, and the 2012 Delhi case is cited as the landmark instance shaping public and legal conscience on women's safety.

Accountability and Ethical Governance

  • PRAGATI — 'Pro-Active Governance and Timely Implementation' — a real-time monitoring platform for government project implementation and grievance redressal, used directly by the PM's office.
  • CPGRAMS — 'Centralized Public Grievance Redress and Monitoring System' — the Indian government's online portal for citizens to lodge grievances against public authorities.
  • Social audit — A participatory accountability tool where local communities review and verify government scheme implementation (e.g. under MGNREGA) — example of bottom-up accountability.

Emotional Intelligence — Concepts and Application in Administration and Governance

  • Communal riots, disaster management — Cited as situations where an administrator's emotional intelligence (calm decision-making under stress, empathy with affected people) is critical.
  • Administrators noted for emotional intelligence in crisis — A category worth building over time — officers cited for calm, empathetic decision-making during disasters, riots, or other high-stress administrative situations.
  • Field-level improvisation during disaster response — A useful general theme: officers using quick, practical, low-cost interventions (such as makeshift flood barriers) under pressure — emotional intelligence showing up as composed, resourceful action rather than panic or rigid procedure-following.
  • Gandhi, Vallabhbhai Patel, V. P. Menon, Dhoni — Cited as figures known for emotional composure and steady decision-making under pressure (Patel and Menon specifically for the calm, persistent diplomacy used in integrating princely states; Dhoni for composure in high-pressure cricket finishes).
  • V. P. Menon and Sardar Vallabhbhai Patel (SVP) — Cited together for their joint role in the political integration of India's princely states post-independence — a model of patient, emotionally intelligent negotiation under huge pressure.
  • Ajit Doval — Mizoram MNF surrender — As Intelligence Bureau officer, Doval met Mizo National Front chief Laldenga; six of seven top MNF commanders chose to return to Delhi with him, which broke Laldenga's resolve and led to the insurgency's peaceful end — example of psychological persuasion and patient field-craft in conflict resolution.
  • Bismarck — Unification of Germany — Cited for shrewd, patient political strategy (realpolitik) — relevant to EQ in high-stakes negotiation and coalition-building.
  • Fateh Singh Rathore — tribal rehabilitation — Tiger conservationist instrumental in establishing Ranthambore National Park, known for sensitively managing the relocation and rehabilitation of local tribal communities — example of balancing conservation goals with empathy for displaced people.
  • Neerja Bhanot — Operation Airlift — Flight attendant who saved hundreds of passengers during the 1986 Pan Am hijacking before losing her own life — example of extraordinary composure and selflessness under extreme pressure.
  • Dandakaranya, Kerala floods — Noted as additional EQ-relevant examples (likely tribal rehabilitation and disaster-response contexts respectively) — flagged for more context if you have it.

Contributions of Moral Thinkers and Philosophers — Indian Values

  • Ahimsa Paramo Dharma — Sanskrit principle meaning 'non-violence is the highest duty/religion' — foundational to Gandhian and Jain ethical thought.
  • Sheelam Param Bhushanam — Sanskrit verse meaning 'character is the greatest ornament' — used to stress that conduct and integrity outweigh wealth, status, or any external adornment.
  • Sarvajan Hitay — 'For the welfare of all' — a foundational Indian value phrase used in public service and Buddhist ethical contexts.
  • Sa Vidya Ya Vimuktaye — 'That alone is knowledge which liberates' — classical Indian view that true education/knowledge should be transformative, not just informative.
  • Satyamev Jayate — 'Truth alone triumphs' — from the Mundaka Upanishad, also India's national motto.
  • Nishkam Karma — 'Action without attachment to the fruits of action' — a central teaching of the Bhagavad Gita, often cited as the ideal mindset for selfless public service.
  • Other philosophers and thinkers worth keeping in this list — Build this out over time with figures such as Swami Vivekananda (service and self-realisation), Mahatma Gandhi (truth and non-violence), Confucius (duty and social harmony), Immanuel Kant (categorical imperative, duty-based ethics), Aristotle (virtue ethics), and Amartya Sen (ethics of justice and capability) — a mix of Indian and Western thinkers commonly drawn on in GS4 answers.

Strengthening Ethical and Moral Values in Governance

Ethical Issues in International Relations and Funding

Positive/cooperative examples

  • Political — Gujral Doctrine — India's 1990s foreign policy doctrine of extending unilateral goodwill and non-reciprocity to smaller neighbours, without expecting matching concessions in return.
  • Political — Panchsheel — The Five Principles of Peaceful Coexistence, agreed between India and China in 1954, used as a model of ethical inter-state conduct.
  • Political — Indus Waters Treaty (1960) — India-Pakistan treaty on sharing river waters, cited as an example of cooperation persisting despite political hostility.
  • Political — NAM (Non-Aligned Movement) — India's Cold War-era foreign policy stance of not formally aligning with either power bloc, on ethical/sovereignty grounds.
  • Political — No First Use, support to Palestine — India's nuclear policy of not striking first; and India's traditional diplomatic support for Palestinian statehood — both cited as principle-based foreign policy positions.
  • Economy — Neo-colonialism, China's debt-trap policy — Criticism of how some powerful states use economic leverage (loans, infrastructure financing) to gain political control over weaker states.
  • Economy — MNCs, protectionism — Ethical tension between multinational corporations' profit motives and a country's need to protect domestic industry/labour.
  • Culture — Tagore's internationalism, Vasudhaiva Kutumbakam — Tagore's vision of a borderless, humanist world culture; and the Sanskrit principle 'the world is one family' — both cited as Indian ethical contributions to global thought.
  • Culture — universal brotherhood, multiculturalism — Cited as ethical ideals for managing a diverse, interconnected world.

Critique/failure examples

  • East Timor invasion — 'no one reacted' — Cited as an example of selective international concern, where a serious violation drew little global response.
  • Biased NPT, denuclearisation — The Non-Proliferation Treaty is criticised as unequal, entrenching the position of existing nuclear powers while restricting others.
  • Institutional bias — UN, World Bank, IMF — These institutions are criticised for reflecting historical power imbalances (e.g. UN Security Council veto structure, voting-share skew at the IMF/World Bank).
  • Global commons — Arctic, climate change, space weapons — Areas requiring shared ethical stewardship beyond narrow national interest, often undermined by competitive national behaviour.
  • Unilateral sanctions by US, withdrawal from UNESCO — Cited as examples of powerful states acting unilaterally in ways that bypass multilateral ethical norms.
  • Refugee crisis, White Australia Policy — The historically racially discriminatory Australian immigration policy, cited alongside the broader ethics of migration and refugee policy.
  • Apartheid, espionage — Apartheid as a historical example of state-sanctioned discrimination; espionage cited for the ethical grey area of state surveillance/spying on other nations.
  • State-sponsored terrorism — Cited as a severe breach of international ethical norms.
  • China — UNCLOS violation, South China Sea (SCS) — China's actions in the South China Sea are cited as a contemporary example of a state disregarding international maritime law (the UN Convention on the Law of the Sea).
