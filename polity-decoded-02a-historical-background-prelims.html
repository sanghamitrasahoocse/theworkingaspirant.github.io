Polity Decoded · Chapter 1 · Prelims Edition

Historical background of the Constitution — every feature, every fact

Sanghamitra S.  ·  June 2026  ·  Prelims reference · 15 min read

This post is designed to replace your book for Prelims purposes. Every Act, every feature, every name and number UPSC has historically tested — in one place. Read it once with attention. Revise it the night before.

The chapter covers two periods: Company Rule (1773–1858) and Crown Rule (1858–1947). There are thirteen Acts in total. For each, what follows is the complete list of features as Laxmikanth presents them — with examiner flags where UPSC has tested that specific detail in past papers.

Company Rule · 1773–1858

1773 Regulating Act
First step by the British Government to control the Company; first recognition of its political functions; laid foundations of central administration in India.

Designated the Governor of Bengal as Governor-General of Bengal; created an Executive Council of four members to assist him. PYQ favourite
First Governor-General of Bengal: Lord Warren Hastings

Made Governors of Bombay and Madras subordinate to the Governor-General of Bengal — previously all three presidencies were independent of one another.

Provided for establishment of a Supreme Court at Calcutta (1774) — comprising one Chief Justice and three other judges. PYQ favourite

Prohibited Company servants from engaging in private trade or accepting presents or bribes from natives.

Strengthened British Government control over the Company — required the Court of Directors to report on revenue, civil, and military affairs in India.

1781 Amending Act Also known as Act of Settlement
Passed to rectify defects of the Regulating Act of 1773 — primarily resolved jurisdictional conflicts between the Supreme Court and the Company.

Exempted the Governor-General, Council members, and Company servants from the jurisdiction of the Supreme Court for official acts.

Excluded revenue matters and matters arising in collection of revenue from the Supreme Court's jurisdiction.

Required the Supreme Court to administer personal law of defendants — Hindus tried under Hindu law, Muslims under Mohammedan law.

Provided that appeals from Provincial Courts go to the Governor-General-in-Council, not to the Supreme Court.

Empowered the Governor-General-in-Council to frame regulations for Provincial Courts and Councils.

1784 Pitt's India Act
Distinguished commercial from political functions of the Company; gave the British Government supreme control over Company's affairs and administration in India.

Distinguished between the commercial and political functions of the Company.

Created a new body called the Board of Control to manage political affairs; Court of Directors retained commercial affairs. Established a system of double government. PYQ favourite

Empowered the Board of Control to supervise and direct all civil, military, and revenue operations of British possessions in India.

Company's territories in India were for the first time called "British possessions in India". PYQ favourite

1786 Act of 1786
Enacted specifically to meet the demands of Lord Cornwallis before accepting the post of Governor-General of Bengal.

Gave the Governor-General power to override the decision of his Council in special cases.

Allowed the Governor-General to also serve as Commander-in-Chief.

1793 Charter Act
Extended and consolidated the powers established in previous Acts; notably made Indian revenues fund British oversight.

Extended the overriding power (first given to Cornwallis) to all future Governor-Generals and Governors of Presidencies.

Gave the Governor-General more powers and control over the subordinate Presidencies of Bombay and Madras.

Extended the trade monopoly of the Company in India for another twenty years.

Provided that the Commander-in-Chief was not to be a member of the Governor-General's Council unless specifically appointed as such.

Members of the Board of Control and their staff were henceforth to be paid out of Indian revenues.

1813 Charter Act
Abolished Company's trade monopoly in India; asserted British Crown's sovereignty over Company territories.

Abolished the trade monopoly of the Company in India — Indian trade thrown open to all British merchants. However, monopoly over trade in tea and trade with China continued.

Asserted the sovereignty of the British Crown over Company territories in India.

Allowed Christian missionaries to come to India for the purpose of enlightening the people.

Provided for the spread of western education among the inhabitants of British territories in India.

Authorised Local Governments to impose taxes on persons and to punish those who did not pay.

1833 Charter Act
Final step towards centralisation in British India. The most constitutionally significant of the Company-era Charter Acts.

Made the Governor-General of Bengal the Governor-General of India — vested with all civil and military powers over entire British territory in India. PYQ favourite
First Governor-General of India: Lord William Bentinck

Deprived the Governors of Bombay and Madras of their legislative powers. The Governor-General of India was given exclusive legislative powers for all of British India.

Laws made under previous acts were called Regulations; laws made under this Act were called Acts.

Ended the Company's commercial activities — it became a purely administrative body. Territories held "in trust for His Majesty, His heirs and successors".

Attempted to introduce open competition for civil servants and stated Indians should not be debarred from any office — but this provision was negated by the Court of Directors.

1853 Charter Act
Last of the series of Charter Acts (1793–1853). A significant constitutional landmark — separated legislative from executive functions for the first time.

Separated, for the first time, the legislative and executive functions of the Governor-General's council. Added six new legislative councillors — creating a separate Governor-General's Legislative Council (also called the Indian Central Legislative Council). PYQ favourite

This legislative council functioned as a mini-Parliament, adopting the same procedures as the British Parliament. Legislation was for the first time treated as a special function requiring special machinery.

Introduced an open competition system for selection and recruitment of civil servants — covenanted civil service thrown open to Indians. The Macaulay Committee (Committee on the Indian Civil Service) was appointed in 1854. PYQ favourite

Extended the Company's rule but did not specify any particular period — unlike previous Charters. A clear signal that the Company's rule could be terminated at any time.

Introduced, for the first time, local representation in the Central Legislative Council — four of the six new legislative members appointed by local governments of Madras, Bombay, Bengal and Agra.

Crown Rule · 1858–1947

1858 Government of India Act Act for the Good Government of India
Enacted in the wake of the Revolt of 1857. Abolished the East India Company and transferred power to the British Crown.

India henceforth to be governed in the name of Her Majesty. Changed designation of Governor-General of India to Viceroy of India — direct representative of the British Crown. PYQ favourite
First Viceroy of India: Lord Canning

Ended the system of double government — abolished the Board of Control and Court of Directors.

Created a new office of Secretary of State for India — vested with complete authority over Indian administration; member of the British Cabinet; responsible ultimately to the British Parliament.

Established a 15-member Council of India to assist the Secretary of State. Advisory body; Secretary of State was its Chairman. PYQ favourite

Constituted the Secretary of State-in-Council as a body corporate — capable of suing and being sued in India and in England.

1861 Indian Councils Act
Important landmark in constitutional history — made a beginning of representative institutions by associating Indians with law-making for the first time.

Made a beginning of representative institutions — Viceroy to nominate some Indians as non-official members of his expanded council. PYQ favourite
Lord Canning nominated three Indians in 1862: Raja of Benaras, Maharaja of Patiala, Sir Dinkar Rao

Initiated decentralisation by restoring legislative powers to Bombay and Madras — reversed the centralising tendency that had peaked under the Charter Act of 1833.

Provided for establishment of new legislative councils for Bengal (1862), North-Western Provinces (1886) and Punjab (1897).

Gave recognition to the portfolio system introduced by Lord Canning in 1859 — each council member in-charge of one or more departments, authorised to issue final orders on those matters.

Empowered the Viceroy to issue ordinances without concurrence of the legislative council during emergencies — life of such ordinance: six months. PYQ favourite

1892 Indian Councils Act
Made a limited and indirect provision for the use of election — the word "election" was not used; the process was described as nomination made on the recommendation of certain bodies.

Increased the number of additional (non-official) members in central and provincial legislative councils — but maintained the official majority.

Increased the functions of legislative councils — gave them the power of discussing the budget and addressing questions to the executive. PYQ favourite
Note: System of Budget was introduced in British India in 1860.

Provided for nomination of non-official members on the recommendation of provincial legislative councils, Bengal Chamber of Commerce, district boards, municipalities, universities, trade associations, zamindars and chambers — election in practice, nomination in name.

1909 Indian Councils Act Morley-Minto Reforms
Lord Morley = Secretary of State for India. Lord Minto = Viceroy. Introduced communal representation — one of the most consequential and controversial provisions in colonial constitutional history.

Considerably increased the size of legislative councils — central legislative council raised from 16 to 60 members. PYQ favourite

Retained official majority in the Central legislative council but allowed non-official majority in provincial legislative councils.

Enlarged deliberative functions — members allowed to ask supplementary questions and move resolutions on the budget.

For the first time, provided for the association of Indians with the executive councils of the Viceroy and Governors. PYQ favourite
First Indian to join the Viceroy's Executive Council: Satyendra Prasad Sinha — appointed as Law Member

Introduced a system of communal representation for Muslims — Muslim members to be elected only by Muslim voters (separate electorate). PYQ favourite
Lord Minto = Father of Communal Electorate

Also provided for separate representation of presidency corporations, chambers of commerce, universities and zamindars.

1919 Government of India Act Montagu-Chelmsford Reforms · came into force 1921
Montagu = Secretary of State. Chelmsford = Viceroy. On August 20, 1917, the British Government declared for the first time that its objective was the gradual introduction of responsible government in India.

Classified all subjects of administration into central subjects and provincial subjects via "Devolution Rules" — relaxed central control over provinces. Both legislatures could make laws on their respective subjects.

Further divided provincial subjects into transferred and reserved:
— Transferred (with ministers): public health, education, local self-government, agriculture
— Reserved (without ministers): police, justice, prisons, land revenue, finance
This dual scheme of governance was called dyarchy (from Greek di-arche = double rule). Largely unsuccessful. PYQ favourite

Introduced, for the first time, bicameralism and direct elections in the country. The Indian Legislative Council replaced by a bicameral legislature: Upper House (Council of State) and Lower House (Legislative Assembly). PYQ favourite

Required that three of the six members of the Viceroy's Executive Council (other than the Commander-in-Chief) were to be Indian.

Extended communal representation — separate electorates for Sikhs, Indian Christians, Anglo-Indians and Europeans.

Granted franchise to a limited number of people on the basis of property, tax or other qualifications.

Created a new office of the High Commissioner for India in London — transferred to him some functions hitherto performed by the Secretary of State.

Provided for the establishment of a public service commission. A Central Public Service Commission was set up in 1926 (on the recommendation of the Lee Commission, 1923–24). PYQ favourite

Separated, for the first time, provincial budgets from the Central budget — authorised provincial legislatures to enact their own budgets.

Provided for the appointment of a statutory commission to report on its working after ten years — this became the Simon Commission.

Proposed the establishment of a Chamber of Princes (Narendra Mandal) — inaugurated in 1921; consisted of 120 members (108 princes + 12 representatives); headed by the Viceroy.

Simon Commission (1927): All seven members were British — hence boycotted by all parties. Submitted its report in 1930. Recommended abolition of dyarchy, extension of responsible government in provinces, and establishment of a federation. Three Round Table Conferences followed; their deliberations led to the Government of India Act 1935.

Communal Award (1932): Ramsay MacDonald extended separate electorates to Depressed Classes (Scheduled Castes). Gandhi undertook a fast unto death in Yerawada Jail. The resulting Poona Pact between Congress and Ambedkar retained the Hindu joint electorate but gave reserved seats to Depressed Classes. Provisions of the Poona Pact were incorporated in the 1935 Act.

1935 Government of India Act
A lengthy and detailed document — 321 sections and 10 schedules. Marked a second milestone towards completely responsible government. The most direct ancestor of the Indian Constitution.

Provided for an All-India Federation of provinces and princely states — but never came into being as princely states did not join. PYQ favourite
Divided powers into three lists: Federal List (59 items), Provincial List (54 items), Concurrent List (36 items). Residuary powers vested in the Viceroy.

Abolished dyarchy in provinces; introduced provincial autonomy — provinces to act as autonomous units. Introduced responsible government in provinces (Governor to act on advice of ministers responsible to provincial legislature). Came into effect in 1937; discontinued in 1939.

Provided for dyarchy at the Centre (federal subjects divided into reserved and transferred) — but this provision never came into operation.

Introduced bicameralism in six out of eleven provinces: Bengal, Bombay, Madras, Bihar, Assam, United Provinces. PYQ favourite

Reserved a certain number of general seats for the Depressed Classes (Scheduled Castes). Extended special representation to women and labour.

Abolished the Council of India (established by the 1858 Act). Secretary of State for India provided with a team of advisors instead.

Extended franchise — about 14 per cent of the total population got the voting right.

Provided for the establishment of a Reserve Bank of India to control the currency and credit of the country. PYQ favourite

Provided for a Federal Public Service Commission, Provincial Public Service Commission, and Joint Public Service Commission for two or more provinces.

Provided for the establishment of a Federal Court — set up in 1937. PYQ favourite

Separated Burma (now Myanmar) from India.

Created two new provinces: Orissa and Sind. PYQ favourite

Provided for safeguards to protect the interests of minorities.

Provided for the establishment of a Federal Railway Authority.

Provided for the appointment of an Auditor-General of India to audit the accounts of the federation and the provinces.

1947 Indian Independence Act
Followed the Mountbatten Plan (June 3, 1947). Indian Independence Bill introduced in British Parliament on July 4, 1947; received Royal Assent on July 18, 1947; came into force on August 15, 1947.

Ended British rule in India — declared India an independent state from August 15, 1947.

Provided for the partition of India and creation of two independent dominions — India and Pakistan — with the right to secede from the British Commonwealth.

Abolished the office of Viceroy. Provided for a Governor-General for each dominion — to be appointed by the British King on the advice of the dominion cabinet. PYQ favourite
First Governor-General of India: Lord Mountbatten
He swore in Jawaharlal Nehru as the first Prime Minister of independent India.

Empowered the Constituent Assemblies of both dominions to frame and adopt any constitution for their respective nations and to repeal any Act of the British Parliament, including the Independence Act itself.

Empowered Constituent Assemblies to legislate for their territories until new constitutions were drafted. No British Act passed after August 15, 1947 was to extend to either dominion unless extended by its own legislature.

Abolished the office of Secretary of State for India — functions transferred to the Secretary of State for Commonwealth Affairs.

Proclaimed the lapse of British paramountcy over Indian princely states and treaty relations with tribal areas from August 15, 1947.

Granted freedom to Indian princely states to join the Dominion of India, join Pakistan, or remain independent.

Provided for the governance of each dominion and the provinces by the Government of India Act 1935 until new constitutions were framed — with authority to make modifications.

Deprived the British Monarch of his right to veto bills or ask for reservation of certain bills for his approval. This right was reserved for the Governor-General.

Designated the Governor-General and provincial Governors as constitutional (nominal) heads — to act on the advice of the respective council of ministers in all matters.

Dropped the title of Emperor of India from the royal titles of the King of England. PYQ favourite

Discontinued the appointment to civil services and reservation of posts by the Secretary of State for India. Members of civil services appointed before August 15, 1947 would continue to enjoy all entitled benefits.

Test yourself — close the page first

1

Which Act first used the term "British possessions in India" and what body did it create to manage political affairs?

2

Name the three Indians Lord Canning nominated to his legislative council in 1862.

3

The Indian Councils Act 1892 introduced indirect elections without using the word "election." What was the process called, and which bodies made recommendations?

4

What were the transferred and reserved subjects under the dyarchy introduced by the 1919 Act? Why did the experiment fail?

5

The Government of India Act 1935 had 321 sections. Name any six institutions it provided for that are still functional in India today.

6

Who was the Father of Communal Electorate and under which Act was the principle introduced?

7

What were the item counts in the three legislative lists under the 1935 Act?

8

The Indian Independence Act 1947 empowered Constituent Assemblies to repeal any British Act — including the Independence Act itself. What does this tell you about the nature of sovereignty it conferred?
